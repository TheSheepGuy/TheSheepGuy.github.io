Sonata (Allegro) Form

Sonata Allegro Form, or just Sonata Form, was invented during the classical period, and was used as the structure for the first movement of a sonata. It has an exposition, a development, and a recapitulation. The exposition has two themes, the first in the tonic key and the second in a different one; and repeats before the development. The development hasn't got a set form, it kinda does whatever it wants, hops from key to key, tempo to tempo, or doesn't. Finally, the recapitulation has the first theme, and the second in the tonic key again; which makes it sound different, but final.

Rondo Form

Rondo Form is a fun form that has one theme alternating with others. Usually, this occurs in three ways: ‘first rondo’, A-B-A; ‘second rondo’, A-B-A-C-A; and ‘third rondo’, A-B-A-C-A-B-A.

Sonata Rondo Form

As the name implies, this is a fusion of sonata, and rondo form. Now, in the development, you repeat the first theme of the expo. (A), therefore making a first rondo and a sonata; sonata rondo form.

Variations

Variations are pieces that are based off of a, most of the time original, melody. This original melody is called the Theme. The others are variations upon the theme. Variations are usually marked with roman numerals

Ternary

Ternary structure is the easiest to understand. An A theme plays first, followed by a B theme, and succeeded by an A theme again. The difference between this and a first rondo, is that a first rondo has drastically different themes; whereas a ternary piece has somewhat related themes.

Rag Structure

Rags usually had a similar structure. Often in ragtime, instead of 'themes' the word 'strains' was used. There are usually four strains, each repeated twice; and the first one once in the middle. So that would be AA BB A CC DD.
