How To Work Out Intervals

Work Out a Note from an Interval

  • If it's a major or minor interval, and it's not a second, take the scale and look for the scale degree mentioned. For example, a minor 6th from F:
    1. It's a major/minor interval, and isn't a second
    2. It's a minor interval. So build an F minor scale: F G A♭ B♭ C D♭ E♭ (F).
    3. And then look at the 6th scale degree. F G A♭ B♭ C D♭, and we've reached the 6th.
    4. Therefore, a minor 6th up from F is D♭.
  • If it's a perfect interval, you can use either scale and look at the degree mentioned. For example, a perfect 4th from B♭:
    1. It's a perfect interval, so either scale can be used.
    2. Let's go for a major scale. B♭ C D E♭ F G A (B♭)
    3. And then look at the 4th scale degree. B♭ C D E♭, and we've reached the 4th.
    4. Therefore, a perfect 4th up from B♭ is E♭.
  • If it's a major or minor second, then you need to apply a different method:
    • If it's a major second, go up a whole step.
    • If it's a minor second, go up a half step.

Work Out an Interval from a Note

For example, work out the interval between B and A. Here, I start with the major scale, but you can also start with the minor.

  1. Take the original note and build a major scale off of it. So, B C♯ D♯ E F♯ G♯ A♯ (B).
  2. Look for the other note and see whether it's in the scale. If it isn't, then use the minor scale. In this case, A isn't there, only A♯. So let's built the minor scale from B: B C♯ D E F♯ G A (B).
  3. Now, look at which scale degree it lands on. Here, A is the on the 7th position.
  4. Then, take the name of the scale and the scale degree, and combine them; in this case it would be a minor 7th.
